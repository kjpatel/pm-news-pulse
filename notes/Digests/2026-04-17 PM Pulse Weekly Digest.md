## This Week

**AI has collapsed the discovery-to-deployment cycle—PMs must now own both product thinking and production automation, or become obsolete.**

This week reveals a fundamental shift in product leadership: AI isn't just a feature to build, it's a capability that collapses traditional PM roles. The tension is stark—discovery (prototyping, validation) remains the bottleneck, yet AI tools are enabling PMs themselves to ship code and automate workflows without engineering. Simultaneously, enterprise AI is fragmenting into distinct architectural patterns, GPU scarcity is reshaping competitive dynamics, and hiring/interviewing practices are evolving faster than most teams can adapt. For a Series C VP, the implication is clear: your org chart and skill mix are misaligned with how product work actually gets done now. Product sense matters more than project management, but execution velocity—knowing which AI agent pattern to deploy and how to integrate it—has become table stakes.

- PMs are now part-time builders—AI tools let you ship prototypes and automation directly, forcing a reckoning with what 'product management' actually means
- AI agents aren't monolithic; deterministic, reasoning, and multi-agent architectures require different go-to-market strategies and success metrics
- GPU scarcity and compute opportunity costs are inverting competitive advantage—scale no longer wins automatically; selectivity and positioning do
- Enterprise implementation (not just selling software) is becoming the defensible, high-margin moat for B2B platforms in the AI era

### 2. [Not all AI agents are created equal](https://www.lennysnewsletter.com/p/not-all-ai-agents-are-created-equal)
*Lenny's Newsletter* — Lenny Rachitsky (featuring Hamza Farooq and Jaya Rajwani) — Apr 14, 2026  `#Agentic`  `#Roadmapping`  `#Enterprise`

Not all AI agents are the same, and treating them as interchangeable is a common Series C mistake. Hamza Farooq and Jaya Rajwani outline three distinct patterns—deterministic automation (scheduled, predictable), reasoning and acting (agentic decision-making), and multi-agent networks (orchestrated workflows)—each with different ROI profiles, implementation timelines, and team requirements. Understanding which type solves your customer problem prevents over-engineering and misallocated resources. This clarity is critical for enterprise PLG: different agent types unlock different expansion revenue motions.

**Why it matters**: Essential framework for your AI/ML product roadmapping: categorizing agent initiatives into three types changes resource planning, timelines, and success metrics.

- **Categorize before prioritizing**: Classify every agent idea into one of three architectural types (deterministic automation, reasoning/acting, or multi-agent networks) before using impact-vs-effort matrices, as comparing different agent architectures on the same spreadsheet leads to poor planning decisions.
- **Start with Category 1 (deterministic automation)**: Begin your agent roadmap with deterministic automation projects using tools like n8n or Zapier, as these deliver quick ROI, require the fewest resources, and can ship in 6 weeks with minimal team overhead.
- **Match tools to architecture type**: Select the appropriate platform based on agent category—n8n/Zapier for workflows, LangGraph/CrewAI for reasoning agents, and ADK/AutoGen for multi-agent systems—rather than forcing all agent types into a single tool.
- **Avoid overengineering Category 1 problems**: Resist the temptation to build deterministic automation workflows with reasoning agent frameworks (Category 2), as this adds unnecessary complexity and cost without adding value.
- **Establish category-specific success metrics**: Define ROI frameworks and KPIs tailored to each architectural type, since a 6-week deterministic automation project and a 6-month multi-agent system have fundamentally different success criteria and measurement approaches.

- **The PM role is dying**: As AI accelerates development cycles, traditional product management is being replaced by faster iteration, design-led approaches, and data-driven decision making without extensive planning.
