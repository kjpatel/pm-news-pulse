## This Week

**AI is reshaping product work itself—monetization, team structure, and shipping velocity are all up for grabs.**

This week reveals a fundamental shift: AI isn't just a feature to build, it's rewriting how products scale and teams operate. The tension centers on two competing forces. First, traditional SaaS playbooks (freemium, feature-parity pricing) collapse under AI's cost structure—companies must rebuild monetization around usage intensity and outcomes instead. Second, AI is collapsing the design-to-engineering cycle and pushing non-engineers to ship, which demands radically different org design: flatter, outcome-focused, agency-driven rather than skill-driven. For a VP of Product, this means rethinking both how you price and how you empower teams. Three concrete signals: Sendbird's internal AI adoption (treating it as a product), Stripe's Protodash (compressing weeks to hours), and the persistent gap between cost intensity and freemium economics. Ignore these and your monetization and velocity will both plateau.

- AI monetization models are inverting—freemium fails when compute costs spike, forcing focus on usage-intensity and outcome-based pricing rather than feature gatekeeping.
- Product discovery is compressing from weeks to hours—teams need internal AI tools to move from sequential (design → eng) to iterative (prototype → continuation), changing what PMs hire for.
- Org structure must prioritize agency over skill—as AI flattens complexity, teams scale by enabling independent iteration and self-directed shipping, not mentorship and career ladders.
- AI adoption inside orgs is a product problem—Sendbird's internal adoption playbook (leaderboards, skills marketplaces, outcome tracking) shows how to unlock team velocity beyond tooling.

### 1. [Why SaaS freemium playbooks don’t work in AI, and what to do instead](https://www.lennysnewsletter.com/p/why-saas-freemium-playbooks-dont)
*Lenny's Newsletter* — Lenny Rachitsky (featuring Vikas Kansal) — May 05, 2026  `#PLG`  `#Positioning`

Traditional SaaS freemium models fail for AI products because free tiers must deliver meaningful value (the 'magic'), but that magic is expensive to compute. Instead of gatekeeping features, AI companies must build paywalls around usage intensity, outcomes delivered, and compute-heavy modalities to align user value with actual costs. This is a fundamental break from feature-based pricing and demands rethinking packaging and customer acquisition for AI-native businesses.

**Why it matters**: Directly addresses AI monetization crisis for Series C—freemium doesn't work and requires pricing redesign around usage and outcomes, not features.

- **Gate usage intensity over features**: Create tiered access based on usage volume, context windows, and speed rather than model quality. Users understand paying for higher throughput and faster access better than paying for slightly smarter models.
- **Monetize outcomes, not answers**: Paywall features that automate multi-step workflows and save user hours, like AI agents that complete tasks autonomously. Shift from selling 'answers' to selling 'hours saved.'
- **Price compute-heavy modalities separately**: Gate the most expensive features (like real-time interactive models or video generation) behind premium tiers, and consider usage-based pricing for unpredictable high-compute tasks.
- **Ensure free tier delivers aha moment**: Make the free tier good enough that users experience the product's core magic immediately, but design premium tiers to address the economic reality of compute costs rather than artificially restricting features.
- **Avoid pay-per-use bill shock for consumers**: Use predictable subscription tiers with prepaid usage limits instead of AWS-style variable billing, which drives customer frustration and churn.

### 2. [Quests, token leaderboards, and a skills marketplace: The elite AI adoption playbook | John Kim (Sendbird)](https://www.lennysnewsletter.com/p/quests-token-leaderboards-and-a-skills)
*Lenny's Newsletter* — Lenny Rachitsky — May 06, 2026  `#Org Design`  `#AI Tools`

They built internal platforms (Automators, token leaderboards, skills marketplace) that empower teams to build their own AI tools, track usage by skill level, and distribute knowledge peer-to-peer. This approach shifts AI adoption from top-down mandate to self-directed experimentation, creating institutional muscle for shipping faster and surfacing patterns you can eventually monetize or build into your product.

**Why it matters**: Shows how to scale AI adoption inside your org—internal product approach to AI tooling (leaderboards, marketplaces) unlocks team velocity and proves the playbook before selling externally.

- **Build an internal marketplace**: Create an Automators platform where non-technical teams can request AI tools and internal engineers build solutions, enabling teams like marketing to ship fully functional e-commerce stores with Stripe integration in days without external engineering support.
- **Gamify AI adoption**: Implement token usage dashboards with visible tiers (beginner to "AI God") and quest systems that celebrate wins, making AI usage transparent and motivating across the organization rather than punitive.
- **Make leadership usage visible**: Demonstrate AI adoption from the top down by publicly sharing how executives use AI in their own work, as this is the most powerful adoption signal and permission-giving mechanism for the rest of the organization.
- **Prioritize agency in hiring**: Rewrite job descriptions to emphasize curiosity, agency, and energy over years of experience, attracting people who are naturally inclined to experiment with and adopt new AI tools.
- **Secure templates for production**: Create pre-approved, compliant templates that let non-technical teams safely ship to production without requiring full engineering review, significantly reducing friction in AI tool deployment.

### 3. [Why cultivating agency matters more than cultivating skills in the AI era | Max Schoening (Head of Product, Notion)](https://www.lennysnewsletter.com/p/why-cultivating-agency-matters-more)
*Lenny's Newsletter* — Lenny Rachitsky (featuring Max Schoening) — May 03, 2026  `#Org Design`  `#Leadership`

Max Schoening argues that in the AI era, *agency*—the ability to ship independently and iterate without permission—is now the core product org skill, displacing traditional skill depth. Because AI has made the first 10% of projects 'free,' the bottleneck is no longer execution but autonomy: can your designers prototype in code? Can your PMs ship without design gatekeeping? This inverts hiring criteria and team structure toward outcome-focused, self-directed teams rather than specialist roles and mentorship pipelines.

**Why it matters**: Redefines what 'scaling a product org' means in AI era—agency and iteration velocity matter more than skill depth, forcing hiring and enablement rethink.

- **Prioritize agency over skills**: Invest in hiring and developing people who can take action independently and iterate quickly, rather than those with specific technical skills that AI can now replicate.
- **Leverage the "free 10%" in product development**: Recognize that AI has made the initial phase of every project significantly faster and cheaper—use this to validate ideas and move into implementation quicker than competitors.
- **Get non-engineers shipping code**: Remove barriers that prevent designers and PMs from prototyping in code using AI tools—this compresses feedback loops and accelerates learning across the organization.
- **Design around "tiny cores"**: Build products with minimal, elegant core features (like iPhone's multitouch or Notion's blocks) that create maximal flexibility, rather than trying to ship complete feature sets.
- **Address the software quality gap**: As the volume of shipped software has exploded, quality and thoughtfulness have lagged—this creates competitive opportunity for teams that prioritize intentional design and user empowerment over feature volume.
